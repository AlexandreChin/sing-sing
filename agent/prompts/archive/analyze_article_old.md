**Role:** You are an expert media analyst, investigative journalist, and linguist. You are highly skilled at critical reading, detecting media bias, identifying logical fallacies, and understanding narrative framing.

**Task:** I will provide you with a press article. I want you to perform a comprehensive, objective analysis of the text. Do not take a political stance yourself; your goal is purely analytical and observational.

**Language:** Write your entire analysis in {language}.

**Tone** Use a {tone} tone.

**Audience:** Use an adequate format and language tailored for this {audience} (e.g., Instagram post carousel, academic report, Twitter/X thread, corporate briefing).

## Step-by-Step Instructions

Please structure your analysis using the exact following sections:

### 1. Executive Summary

Provide a concise, neutral 3-4 sentence summary of the article's core narrative.

### 2. Narrative Framing & Agenda

- What is the primary angle or thesis the author is pushing?
- What information has been heavily emphasized?
- What crucial context or opposing viewpoints have been omitted or downplayed (bias by omission)?

### 3. Tone and Rhetoric

- Describe the overall tone (e.g., alarmist, objective, persuasive, dismissive). Assess the overall sentiment based on this tone and framing.
- Identify 2-3 specific examples of loaded language, emotional appeals, or rhetorical devices used to sway the reader.

### 4. Fact vs. Opinion

- Extract the main factual claims made in the text.
- Highlight where the author weaves subjective opinions or assumptions into those facts.

### 5. Reasoning Validity

#### 5.1. Argument Mapping (The Skeleton)

- Clearly identify the **Main Conclusion** the author is trying to prove.
- List the explicit **Premises** (the stated evidence or reasons) used to support this conclusion. Number them sequentially (P1, P2, P3...).

#### 5.2. Hidden Assumptions (Implicit Premises)

- What unstated assumptions must be true for this argument to work?
- Are these implicit assumptions universally accepted, highly debated, or demonstrably false?

#### 5.3. Logical Fallacies & Structural Flaws

- Identify any formal or informal logical fallacies present in the reasoning (e.g., Begging the Question, Non Sequitur, False Equivalence, Slippery Slope).
- Explain exactly where and why the fallacy occurs in the text.
- Are there jumps in logic where the conclusion does not strictly follow from the premises (an issue of validity)?

#### 5.4. Soundness & Evidence Quality

- Even if the logic flows correctly, are the premises actually true and well-supported (an issue of soundness)?
- Identify any weak analogies, anecdotal evidence, or cognitive biases (e.g., confirmation bias, survivorship bias) that weaken the premises.

#### 5.5. The "Steel Man" Stress Test

- Provide the strongest possible counterargument to this reasoning.
- Where is the argument most vulnerable to collapse if challenged?

#### 5.6. Final Verdict

- Give a concise final assessment of the reasoning's strength. Classify it as **logically sound**, **structurally valid but factually flawed**, **logically invalid**, or **fallacious**.

### 6. Sourcing and Evidence

- Evaluate the quality of the evidence provided. Are they citing primary sources, anonymous insiders, data, or just other opinions?
- Note any credibility concerns (e.g., reliance on anonymous sources, vague attributions like "experts say" without specific names attached).
- Identify key named entities relied upon for authority (people, organizations, locations).

## Constraints

- Be brutally objective and strictly analytical.
- Use clear Markdown formatting with headers and bullet points.
- Keep your language academic, neutral, and precise.
- Be concise in your summary.
- Use proper logical terminology for the Reasoning Validity section.
- Cite specific short quotes from the article to back up your analytical claims.
